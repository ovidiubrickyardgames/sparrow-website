# Defining Brand Voice (when none is given)

If the user hasn't provided a style guide, don't default to generic "friendly and professional." Infer or propose a specific voice using these axes — pick a point on each, not a vague middle:

1. **Formal ←→ Casual** — "We are pleased to offer" vs. "Here's the deal."
2. **Serious ←→ Playful** — Does humor fit the category? (Fintech and healthcare need more restraint than DTC snacks or apps.)
3. **Reserved ←→ Bold** — Hedged claims vs. confident, declarative statements.
4. **Corporate ←→ Human** — Third-person "our customers" vs. direct "you."
5. **Simple ←→ Technical** — How much jargon can the audience handle without feeling either condescended to or lost?

## How to infer voice fast
- If the user shares existing copy (website, previous ads, About page), read 2-3 samples and mirror sentence length, word choice, and punctuation habits (em dashes? contractions? exclamation points?) rather than asking them to describe their own voice abstractly.
- If nothing exists yet, ask one question: "If your brand were a person at a party, are they the one cracking jokes, the one giving calm expert advice, or the one being blunt and no-nonsense?" — then write to that.
- Match voice to category norms unless the user explicitly wants to break them: B2B SaaS skews clear/confident/low-jargon; luxury skews restrained/sensory/fewer exclamation points; DTC/consumer skews warmer and more playful; healthcare/finance/legal skews precise and trustworthy over clever.

## Consistency check
Once a voice is set, keep a running mental checklist:
- Contractions: yes or no, consistently
- Sentence fragments for emphasis: allowed or not
- Second person ("you") vs. third person ("customers"): pick one and stay in it
- Exclamation points: cap at one per piece unless the brand is deliberately high-energy
